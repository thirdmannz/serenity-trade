# Aleabit on Research Process

## Core principles

- **Supply chain mapping FIRST, valuation last.** Before opening a DCF, map the physical flow: raw material → substrate → component → subsystem → end product. Identify which nodes are concentrated and which are commoditized.
- **Use academic + proprietary sources, not just sell-side notes.** Sell-side is usually downstream of the thesis you want to find. Academic literature on materials science, government supply-chain reports, and trade-data sources surface bottlenecks earlier.
- **Multi-order systems thinking.** Don't stop at 1st-order ("policy X hurts company Y"). Trace through to 3rd, 4th, 5th order effects — who substitutes? Who can't? What's the lead time on alternate supply? Where does the pressure relocate?
- **AI pressure-test the logic.** He explicitly uses LLMs (Gemini) to stress-test reasoning chains — feed in your thesis and ask the model to attack it from supply-side, demand-side, geopolitical, and substitution angles.
- **Earnings is the validation gate.** Theory + supply data give the hypothesis; earnings (and customer disclosures) confirm or kill it. Scale conviction only after the print validates.
- **High transparency externally, independent thinking internally.** Share weights and theses publicly to invite challenge — but don't copy others, and don't let others copy without doing the work.

## How to apply

When a user starts diligence on a new name, walk them through this workflow:

**Step 1 — Map the supply chain.**
- What does the company sell?
- What inputs does it require? List them as concrete physical/material inputs.
- For each input: how many global suppliers, what's the geographic concentration, what's the lead time to substitute?

**Step 2 — Identify the chokepoint candidate.**
- Which node(s) have <5 global suppliers, geographic concentration risk, or multi-year substitute lead times?
- Is the company in this chokepoint, downstream of it, or substitutable around it?

**Step 3 — Map customer dependency.**
- Who buys the chokepoint output? Are hyperscalers (NVDA, Broadcom, Marvell, Google ASIC team) confirmed customers via disclosures, supply agreements, or strong public inference?
- What's the design-win lock-in? Once qualified, how long until they could re-qualify a competitor?

**Step 4 — Multi-order stress.**
- 1st order: thesis works → company benefits
- 2nd: who else benefits / loses along the chain?
- 3rd: how does the market respond when the bottleneck becomes consensus?
- 4th: geopolitical exposure — what happens if China restricts the input, or US sanctions a downstream customer?
- 5th: what substitution paths exist on a 3–5 year horizon, and do they invalidate the thesis?

**Step 5 — AI pressure-test.**
- Hand the full thesis to a strong LLM with the prompt: "Attack this from supply, demand, substitution, geopolitical, and valuation angles. List the strongest 5 reasons this is wrong."
- Steelman every counter. If any counter holds up, the thesis is not ready for high-conviction sizing.

**Step 6 — Calendar the validation gate.**
- When is the next earnings print? What specific datapoint would confirm vs falsify the chokepoint thesis (pricing power, capacity disclosures, customer concentration, gross margin trajectory)?
- Pre-commit to the action under each outcome.

Once Step 6 outputs a scaling plan, hand off to [[aleabit-position-sizing]] and [[aleabit-entry-exit]] for execution.
